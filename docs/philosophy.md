# Philosophy

## The pipeline we inherited

Every mainstream methodology of the last fifty years — waterfall, agile, XP, DevOps — refines the same underlying pipeline:

```text
Requirements → Design → Implementation → Testing
```

The stages moved, shrank, and looped, but one assumption never changed: **implementation is the expensive, human, central act**, and everything else exists to support or check it. Design exists so humans implement the right thing; testing exists because human implementation is fallible; code review exists because a second human catches what the first missed.

## What actually changed

AI did not just make coding faster. It inverted the cost structure. Implementation — the historical bottleneck — became the cheapest stage, while precisely what the old pipeline treated as overhead became the expensive, decisive work: **defining what "correct" means, and proving the result meets it.**

Software is going through its industrial revolution. Code was craft: one mind shaping one artifact, quality living in the craftsman's skill and a colleague's inspection. Generation made code machine output — and every craft that industrialized hit the same wall: **the ways you trusted hand-made artifacts do not survive machine production.** You cannot interrogate the machine's intent in review. You cannot inspect your way through machine-scale volume. Manufacturing hit this wall a century ago and engineered its way past it; those solutions are the subject of [industrial-lessons.md](industrial-lessons.md).

## Why a line cannot carry quality

The natural first response to AI is to keep the pipeline and swap the implementer — this is Spec-Driven Development: specification → AI implementation. A line.

A line has two structural defects:

1. **Correctness is implicit.** The spec says what the system does on the intended path. Everything else — under attack, under load, under partial failure, under concurrency — is left to whatever the generator happened to produce. Somebody decided your concurrency semantics, and it wasn't you.
2. **Verification is circular.** Checks derived from the spec, generated by the agent that implemented the spec, confirm the implementation's own assumptions — including its bugs. High coverage, zero independence.

You cannot fix a line by making the spec longer. Correctness and proof are different *kinds* of artifact, not more paragraphs of the same one.

## The triangle

```text
                Specification
               what it must do
                 ╱         ╲
                ╱           ╲
               ╱   ( code )  ╲
              ╱               ╲
        Quality ──────────── Verification
   what "correct" means      what is proven
```

AIQSE promotes correctness and proof to vertices of their own:

- **Specification** defines behavior.
- **Quality** defines correctness, explicitly, across nine dimensions.
- **Verification** derives proof from the other two vertices — never from the implementation it judges. The gauge is not made by the part.

Code moves to the interior: generated, disposable, regenerable, acceptable exactly when the triangle closes around it. Each edge does work — Specification↔Quality (does the definition of correct cover the specified behavior?), Quality↔Verification (does every dimension have an instrument?), Verification↔Specification (does the proof trace back to expected behavior?).

## The triangle is agent-agnostic — and that is the point

Notice what the triangle does *not* mention: people. Nothing requires a human to write the spec, define the quality model, or issue the verdict. Today humans hold most vertices because today's verification isn't yet strong enough to let go of them — a fact about the current level of autonomy ([autonomy-levels.md](autonomy-levels.md)), not about the discipline.

This is the philosophical break with human-centric methodologies. Agile optimized how humans collaborate; SDD optimized how humans instruct machines. AIQSE defines what must be true of the *process* regardless of who operates it — which is the only kind of definition that survives full automation. Manufacturing made the same move: the lights-out factory did not abandon quality engineering; it is quality engineering's final exam.

## What this is not

Not a claim that engineers stop mattering — someone must express intent, set standards, and answer for outcomes; history suggests that role concentrates rather than disappears. Not a rejection of TDD, BDD, or SDD — each identified a real truth; the triangle arranges those truths for a world where the implementer, and eventually the verifier, is a machine.

Nor is it finished. This document names a shift many teams are living through without shared vocabulary — so we can argue about it, refine it, and eventually trust it.
